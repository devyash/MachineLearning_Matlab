                                                                                            Sebastian Ruder Mod Jan En 3 months ago

                                                                                            You define the error on your training set always as the average error over your training examples as in your last equation, to make it independent of your training set size. This is not directly related to averaging your gradient.
                                                                                            The reason we average the gradient over a batch or mini-batch is to obtain more consistent updates: the larger our batcher, the smoother our updates. Remember the picture for stochastic GD: When we compute the gradient for every example, we perform updates in many different directions and thus get the zig-zagging behaviour in our error function. Averaging simply helps to make this behaviour more consistent as large updates in different directions cancel each other out; consequently, the one averaged update we perform (instead of separate updates for each example) is beneficial for most examples in the batch and thus is likely to lead us more directly to a (local) minimum.
